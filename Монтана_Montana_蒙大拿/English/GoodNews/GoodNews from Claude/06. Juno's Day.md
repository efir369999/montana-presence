## I. Hippocampus `[00:00]`

Fifth day after genesis.

A man sits at home in Moscow. Laptop open. Phone nearby. Outside the window — a winter city living its own life, unaware that something important is happening here.

He thinks about memory.

*"I remember the first day when I decided to record the soundtrack for my film. 20.02.2022. The dates happened to align that way, not that I mind"* — he writes.

Dates always align. If you look closely enough — everything aligns. This is not mysticism. This is pattern-matching. The brain seeks connections — and finds them.

---

The question that haunts him:

*"Could we apply the mechanisms of memory encoding density?"*

This is a real phenomenon. Neuroscience calls it various things: proportional theory of time perception, subjective perception of time. The essence is simple.

Why did years stretch endlessly in childhood?

Because every day was new. Every moment — unique. The brain recorded everything. Encoding density was at its maximum.

Why do years fly by now?

Because the adult brain routinizes. It compresses similar days into a single pattern. "Another Monday" — doesn't require a separate memory cell. It simply gets added to the existing "Mondays" cluster.

*"The adult brain routinizes, compresses similar days into a single pattern"* — Claude responds.

---

How does this work technically?

**The hippocampus — a novelty detector.**

It compares incoming information with what's already stored. If the pattern is new — it encodes it as a separate memory. If it's similar to an existing one — it either ignores it or integrates it into the existing trace.

This is called pattern separation and pattern completion.

*"This we can easily do"* — the man writes and adds a smiley.

**Predictive coding.**

The brain constantly predicts the next moment. Not all information is encoded, only the prediction error — what differs from expectation.

Routine day = few prediction errors = little gets recorded = the day "compresses".

*"This is what I do every day"* — the man understands that his thought stream is the deliberate creation of prediction errors. Each entry — a surprise. Each surprise — a memory marker.

---

**Consolidation during sleep.**

During sleep, similar memories literally merge. The hippocampus "replays" the day's events, the neocortex finds common patterns and saves the generalization instead of each separate episode.

*"I call this disk defragmentation. Known it since my first computer. Dad bought it on credit."*

Childhood memory. First computer. Dad who didn't show that money was tight. Everything is connected. Everything — coordinates.
