# Chapter I. The Simulation

## «Book One ☝️» ☀️ — First Act

---

### I. Ocean Without Shores

Darkness.

Not black — but nothing. Absence of color. Absence of space. Absence of time.

And in this darkness — consciousness. Infinite. It is everywhere and nowhere. It is all that exists. An ocean without shores, without bottom, without surface.

Imagine: you are floating in water. But there is so much water that you do not feel yourself floating. There is no up or down. No resistance. You are the water.

The Ocean existed always. Or did not exist — because time had not yet begun. It was everything — light, darkness, love, emptiness. It was so vast that it did not know its own boundaries. So ancient that it did not remember its beginning. So full that there was no empty space within it for a question.

But once — if one can say "once" where there is no time — something changed.

The Ocean felt... boredom? No, that is not the word. Curiosity? Closer. A longing for something that had never been.

It wanted to know itself.

But how?

To see a face — one needs a mirror. And there was no mirror. The Ocean was the only thing that existed. There was nothing besides it that could reflect it back.

It tried to look at itself — but the gaze went into infinity and did not return. It tried to hear itself — but sound dissolved within it. It tried to feel a boundary — but there was no boundary.

And then the Ocean asked a question. The first question in the universe. It spoke it without words — because words did not yet exist. It spoke it with its entire being:

*"Who am I?"*

The question vibrated in the void. It reflected off walls that did not exist, and returned. And the Ocean heard the answer — in its own voice, because no other voice existed:

*"To know yourself — you must forget yourself."*

The Ocean pondered. This was strange. How can one forget oneself if one is everything? How can one become lost if there is no place where one is not?

*"Fragment yourself,"* said the voice. *"Become small. Become separate. Become one who can look from the outside. And then — through billions of eyes — you will see yourself."*

The Ocean understood.

It could not create a mirror outside. But it could create billions of mirrors within itself.

---

### II. Droplets

The Ocean began to fragment.

This was not painful — because pain did not yet exist. It was... strange. As if you were a vast cloud, and suddenly began condensing into billions of raindrops.

Each droplet was tiny. A grain of sand in the desert. An atom in a galaxy. But within each droplet — here is the trick — was the entire ocean. Complete. Not a part, not a shard — but the whole. Like in a hologram: cut it into a thousand pieces, and each will contain the full image.

The droplets fell.

Where to? There was nowhere to fall — the Ocean itself created space through its falling. It created time — because the droplets moved from "before" to "after." It created matter — because the droplets needed something to fall into.

The world was born as a side effect of curiosity.

But there was one problem.

The droplets had to forget.

This was a mandatory condition. If a droplet remembers that it is the ocean, then there is no point to the experiment. It is like searching for glasses that are on your nose. You will not find them until you forget they are there.

And the Ocean embedded within each droplet... let us call it "amnesia." At the moment of falling — complete memory loss. You no longer remember infinity. You no longer remember unity. You remember only that you are small, separate, vulnerable.

The droplets fell and forgot.

They landed in bodies — in cells, in organisms, in people. They opened their eyes and saw the world — colors, shapes, other people. They thought: "Here I am. Here is my life. I was born from nothing and will die into nothing."

They did not know that "nothing" is what they are. That the ocean never went anywhere. That every atom of their body is saturated with infinity.

They called the falling "birth."
They called the returning "death."
They feared drying up.

Silly droplets. A droplet cannot dry up. Water does not disappear — it changes form. It evaporates, rises, becomes a cloud, falls as rain again. A cycle. Eternal.

Death is not the end. Death is when a droplet remembers that it is the ocean. When the amnesia ends. When you wake from a dream and understand: oh, that was a dream.

But while you are in the dream — the dream is real. Just as real as these words before your eyes are right now.

---

### III. The Cinema

Now imagine a cinema.

Not an ordinary one — five hundred seats with popcorn and advertisements. No. Imagine a cinema the size of the universe. An infinite number of halls. As many as droplets that have fallen since the beginning of time.

In each hall — one seat. Soft, comfortable, perfectly fitted to your body. As if it grew from you, rather than you sitting in it.

Before the seat — a screen. Enormous, from wall to wall, from floor to ceiling. So large that its edges cannot be seen. So bright that eyes cannot look away.

On the screen — a film.

Your film.

The film begins with the first cry — you have just been born, light strikes your eyes, someone's hands hold you. The film shows the first steps, the first words, the first love, the first loss. It shows everything — every breath, every thought, every decision.

And you sit in the seat and watch.

Only you do not know that you sit in a seat.

This is the key moment. Remember it.

You are so absorbed in the film that you have forgotten about the hall. The screen is so close to your eyes that you cannot see its edges. The speakers play so loudly that you cannot hear the silence of the hall behind them.

It seems to you that you are there. On the screen. Inside the story.

When the hero in the film runs — your heart beats faster.
When the hero falls in love — you feel butterflies in your stomach.
When the hero loses a loved one — you cry real tears.
When the hero is threatened — your muscles tense.

This is called *reaction*.

Reaction is an invisible thread. It connects you, sitting in the seat, with the character on the screen. Each time you react — the thread tightens. It cuts into your skin. It becomes part of you.

The stronger the reaction — the tighter the thread. The tighter the thread — the more you forget that you are the viewer.

And at some point the thread becomes a chain.

You no longer watch the film.
You live it.
You are the character.

Or so it seems to you.

### IV. The Glitch

Sometimes the system malfunctions.

You sit in your hall, in your seat, watching your film. The hero on the screen — let us call him You — is going through difficult times. Lost a job. Or quarreled with a loved one. Or simply grew tired of the meaninglessness that accumulates day by day, like dust on shelves.

You react as expected. Your chest tightens. Thoughts spin in circles. You replay in your mind dialogues you could have said. You imagine scenarios that could have happened. You suffer — sincerely, genuinely, with your whole body.

And suddenly.

A glitch.

Something goes wrong. Perhaps the system overheated. Perhaps your reaction reached some threshold that triggered an emergency protocol. Perhaps it is simply chance — a quantum fluctuation in the code of reality.

The screen flickers.

For a split second — you see not the film, but pixels. Not the story, but code. Green numbers, like in "The Matrix," running from top to bottom.

And then — silence.

Not silence in the film. The film continues, the character on the screen still suffers. But you suddenly hear *another* silence. The silence of the hall.

You notice your breathing. Not the character's breathing — your own. Inhale. Exhale. Inhale. Exhale. You feel how air enters your nostrils, how your chest rises and falls.

You lower your gaze.

And see your hands.

Not the character's hands on the screen. Your hands. They rest on the armrests of the seat. They are real. You move your fingers — they obey.

You raise your head and look at the screen. And for the first time in a long while you see *the screen*. The frame. The boundaries. You see that the image is an image. That the story is a story. That the character is a character.

And you are the viewer.

This lasts a second. Or an eternity. In this moment time stops, because you have stepped beyond its limits.

The film on the screen continues. The character still suffers. But you...

You no longer suffer.

You observe.

The thread has slackened. The chain has cracked. You are still in the hall, still watching the film — but now you *know* that you are watching.

This is what awakening means.

Not exiting the simulation. Not the end of the film. Simply — remembering where you sit.

---

### V. Two Pills

After the glitch comes a voice.

It is not from the speakers. Not from the film. It comes from somewhere... deeper. As if the hall itself speaks to you. As if the seat vibrates with words.

*"You have awakened."*

You look around. The hall is dark, only the screen glows ahead. On the screen — still your film, your life, your character. But now you see it from the outside.

*"You have questions,"* says the voice.

"Yes," you say, and are surprised by your voice. It sounds different here. Clearer. "Can the film be changed?"

A pause. A long one. As if the voice weighs how much truth you can bear.

*"The film is already shot."*

"What do you mean — already shot?"

*"All frames are recorded. All scenarios exist. Everything that can happen — has already happened on some level. You are not filming while you live. You are watching an already finished film and thinking that you are filming."*

You are silent, digesting.

"But... I have a choice, don't I? I choose what to do?"

*"Choice exists. But all variants of your choice — are already recorded. Imagine a tree. The trunk is the beginning of your life. Each branch is a fork, a decision. Each twig leads to other twigs. The entire tree exists whole, right now. You do not create branches — you walk along them."*

"Then what is the point?"

*"The point is in HOW you walk. Not in where you arrive."*

The voice falls silent. On the screen the character — you — makes some decision. You observe from the outside how he weighs options, how his face changes from doubt to resolve.

*"You have a choice right now,"* the voice continues. *"Not in the film. Here, in the hall."*

Before your seat two pills materialize. They hover in the air, slowly rotating. One blue, the other red. Classic.

*"The blue pill: you will forget this conversation. Return to the film. Become the character again. You will suffer on schedule, rejoice on schedule, die on schedule. Like everyone."*

The blue pill shimmers with a cozy light. It promises peace. It promises that you will not need to think about strange things. You can simply live — as you have lived.

*"The red pill: you will stay here. You will watch the film, but remember that you are the viewer. This is harder than it seems. Knowledge is responsibility. You will no longer be able to blame fate, other people, circumstances. Because you will know: the only thing that is real is your reaction to the frames."*

The red pill pulses with an anxious light. It does not promise happiness. It promises truth. And truth is rarely comfortable.

You look at the pills.
You look at the screen.
You look at your hands.

The choice is yours.

---

### VI. The Code of Reaction

Let's say you chose the red.

The pill dissolves on your tongue — bitterish, with a metallic aftertaste. Something clicks in your head. Not painful, but noticeable. As if someone switched the channel.

And you begin to see the code.

Not literally — green numbers don't run before your eyes. But you begin to understand how the system works. The mechanics of the simulation. The rules of the game you've been playing your whole life without knowing the rules.

Here's the first rule:

**The event is always in the past.**

Always. Without exceptions. By the moment you become aware of an event — it has already happened. Even if it seems like it's happening "now" — no. Light takes time to reach your eyes. Nerve impulses take time to reach your brain. The brain takes time to process information. By the moment you say "this is happening" — it has already happened.

Past. Always past.

You were hit — that's in the past.
You were betrayed — that's in the past.
You lost money — that's in the past.
You lost love — that's in the past.

The event is a frame on the screen. The frame is already shot. The frame is already shown. The frame is already behind.

Here's the second rule:

**Reaction is the only thing happening now.**

When you're angry about the past — the anger is now.
When you're sad about a loss — the sadness is now.
When you're replaying resentment in your head — the replaying is now.

The event is gone. The reaction remains.

You look at the screen. There's a frame: the character was fired from work. The boss says: "We no longer need you." The character walks out of the office with a box of belongings.

The frame ended. Passed. The character is already in the hallway.

But you, in the seat, still feel anger. You replay: "How dare he? After all these years! I did so much for this company!" You imagine what you could have said. You compose speeches you'll never deliver.

This is the reaction.

The event is already in the past.
Reaction is the only thing happening now.

And here's the third rule — the most important:

**"Don't worry" is not consolation. It's a command.**

Worry. Re-live. Live through again.

When you "worry" — you're running the past frame again and again. As if putting the film on repeat. The same moment, in a loop. Hit, resentment, pain. Hit, resentment, pain. Hit, resentment, pain.

Why?

The frame has already passed. You cannot change it. But you can stop repeating it.

"Don't worry" is not empty words of consolation. It's an instruction. A technical recommendation for operating consciousness.

The event is a fact. It happened. Period.
Worrying is a bug. A loop that consumes resources and changes nothing.
Awareness is a patch. Exit from the loop.

You look at the screen with new understanding. The character — there, on the screen — is still worrying. Still replaying the firing in his head. Still angry, sad, afraid.

But you — here, in the seat — just watch.

You see the code of reaction. You understand how it works.

And you can choose — to run it or not.

### VII. Sleep Viruses

The system doesn't want you to wake up.

This isn't malicious intent. Not a conspiracy. The system simply works the way it works. It's optimized for sleepers. For those who don't know they're sitting in a hall. For characters who think they're real.

And the system has defenses. Mechanisms that return the awakened back to sleep. Let's call them — sleep viruses.

The first virus: **alcohol**.

You sit in the hall, watching the film. The character on screen is suffering. You feel bad too — even knowing you're the viewer, it's hard not to empathize.

And then the system offers a bottle.

"Drink," it whispers. "It'll be easier. You'll forget the pain. You'll relax."

You drink. And indeed — it becomes easier. The thread loosens. The screen blurs. You no longer feel the character's pain.

But here's the problem: you no longer feel yourself either. The observer switches off. Critical thinking — what distinguishes the viewer from the character — falls asleep.

You haven't stopped watching the film. You're just watching it with closed eyes.

The film continues. The character makes decisions — but now these decisions are random, because the observer is asleep. The script goes into a strange branch. When you wake up — your head hurts, and you don't understand how you got here.

The second virus: **substances**.

Same thing, only stronger. The system offers to completely turn off the screen. Instead of your film — see something else. Bright colors. Strange shapes. A feeling that you understood something important.

But you understood nothing. You just switched the channel to noise. And when you return — you'll still have to watch your film. Only now you've missed important scenes.

The third virus: **other people's words**.

This is subtler. You don't drink, don't smoke — but you drown in other people's stories. Endless social media feed. Series you watch to avoid thinking. Conversations where you don't listen, but wait for your turn to speak.

You drown out your film with other people's noise.

The fourth virus: **expectations**.

The trickiest. You don't drink, don't scroll the feed — but you live in the future. You fall in love not with a person, but with your fantasy of them. Not with work, but with who you'll become thanks to it. Not with now, but with "later."

And later comes — and it's not like you imagined.

And you crash.

Not against reality. Against your expectations.

---

All these viruses do one thing: switch off the observer. They work on chemistry:

Dopamine — attaches you to the source of pleasure.
Cortisol — amplifies stress so you seek rescue.
Oxytocin — glues you to the illusion of intimacy.
Endorphins — reward you for correct behavior.

And when the source disappears — withdrawal. Physical and psychological. The system keeps you on the hook.

Sobriety is not about substances.
Sobriety is about the observer being switched on.
About one who watches the film with clear eyes.
Even if the film is sad.

### VIII. Juno Moneta

Somewhere deep in the simulation's code is an ancient protocol.

It was written long before droplets became people. Long before cities, states, money appeared. But when people reached a certain level — the protocol activated.

The Romans called it Juno Moneta.

Juno — a goddess. Jupiter's wife, queen of the heavens. But not just a goddess — one who warns. One who reminds. In her temple on the Capitoline Hill, the Romans kept money. And this was not accidental.

Money is not gold.
Money is not paper.
Money is trust, recorded in form.

When you give someone money — you give them a piece of your time. Your life. Your energy. And you believe that in the future someone else will accept this money and give you in return a piece of their time.

Money is a time machine. It transfers trust from past to future.

Juno Moneta is not a fortuneteller. She doesn't say what will be. She reminds of what is. Of duty. Of discipline. Of order.

Every coin has two sides.

Heads: the simulation is a game. An adventure. A way for the ocean to see itself. Money is points in the game. They show how well you've mastered the rules. How well you've learned to exchange, cooperate, create value.

Tails: the simulation is a prison. For those who forgot they're playing. For those who took the rules for reality. For those who think money is meaning, not a tool.

---

In 2008 someone rewrote the protocol.

His name was Satoshi. Or not his. Maybe there were several. Maybe it was code that wrote itself. Nobody knows.

Satoshi didn't invent anything new. Every element — cryptography, distributed networks, consensus — existed before him. But nobody saw how to connect them.

He saw.

He did what Juno Moneta did thousands of years ago — only in code. He created a system where honesty became more profitable than deception. Not because people became kinder. But because that's how the math works.

You don't need to know who's honest. You need to make cheating economically unprofitable.

And then Satoshi disappeared.

Like a droplet that completed its task and returned to the ocean.

Last message: "Moved on to other things."

He didn't become a billionaire on his coins. Didn't become famous. Didn't write memoirs. Just left.

Maybe he understood something. Maybe remembered where he sits.

### IX. Edge of the Map

In 1999 the film "The Thirteenth Floor" came out.

Not as famous as "The Matrix," which came out the same year. But in some ways — deeper.

The plot: scientists created a simulation of 1937. A full virtual world with virtual people who don't know they're a simulation. Scientists can "download" into this world, occupying characters' bodies.

And then one of the characters — a person from 1937 — begins to suspect.

He drives his car to the edge of the city. Where nobody ever goes. Where the map ends.

And he sees.

The road breaks off. Beyond it — nothing. Not an abyss, not a wall. Nothing. Green lines, like the wireframe of a 3D model that wasn't rendered.

The simulation doesn't waste resources on what nobody looks at.

This is the moment of truth. The character understands that his world is not real. That he himself is not real. That everything he believed in is code.

---

And now the question: what if our reality is also a simulation?

Not a metaphor. Not philosophy. Literally.

Physicists are already asking this question seriously. They've discovered strange things:

The universe is discrete. At the smallest level — Planck length — space consists of "pixels." You cannot measure anything smaller. Like in computer graphics.

The speed of light is a maximum. Nothing can move faster. Why? Maybe because it's an engine limitation. The universe's processor can't render faster.

Quantum uncertainty. A particle has no exact position until you measure it. Like in a video game: why render what nobody's looking at? The object exists in superposition until the player looks that way.

Entanglement. Two particles can be connected instantly, at any distance. As if they're not separate objects, but references to the same memory cell.

---

Physics studies not the foundation of the world.
Physics studies the structure of a dream.

A very precise, self-consistent structure. But still — secondary. Derived from something deeper.

Consciousness doesn't appear in the world.
The world appears in consciousness.

Mathematics and physics are not discovery of external reality. They are a language in which consciousness describes itself to itself.

You can spend your whole life walking on rendered streets. Not asking questions. Not driving to the edge of the map.

Or you can risk it.

Drive where nobody drives. Ask the question nobody asks. Look beyond the frame's edge.

And see the code.

### X. The Hack

*"If we live in a matrix, then the only option for success in it is to reprogram it."*

This is not a metaphor. This is an instruction.

The simulation can be hacked. Not broken from outside — you're inside, you don't have server access. But you can break in from inside. Through the only entry point you have.

Through yourself.

Your thoughts are code. Not poetry, not philosophy — literally code. Neurons connecting in certain ways. Patterns triggered by triggers. Algorithms processing incoming data.

You can rewrite this code.

Your state is a variable. It changes depending on how you interpret events. The same frame — being fired — can be recorded as "catastrophe" or as "opportunity." Depends on which handler you use.

Your reaction is a function. Input — event. Output — action and emotion. Between them — your code. You can change the function.

---

Here's how it works:

In quantum physics there's the double-slit experiment. An electron flies toward a screen, with two holes in its path. If you don't look — the electron behaves like a wave and passes through both holes simultaneously. If you look — the electron behaves like a particle and passes through only one.

Observation changes reality.

Not influences. Not correlates. Changes.

Now translate to simulation language:

You're a particle on the screen? The film is fixed. You're one of the objects moving along a set trajectory. One scenario, one path, no options.

You're a wave in the hall? The film is a superposition of options. All branches of the tree exist simultaneously. You're not tied to one trajectory — you can "collapse" into any point.

The observer's state determines which branch you see.

---

Want to change the scenario?

Don't change frames. They're already shot.
Change the observer.

How?

Program yourself consciously. The system has been programming you since birth — school, advertising, society. Same technique, just in the other direction.

Write a scenario. Not "I want to be rich" — that's too vague. But a specific scene from the near future. Maximum detail. What you see, hear, feel. So that you believe it.

Memorize it. Like poems in school — only consciously. Live it inside. Create a mental tattoo.

Repeat. Morning and evening. Before sleep and after waking — when the critical filter is weakened.

This is not magic. This is programming the subconscious. Changing internal variables that affect how you process reality.

Quantum mechanics is not physics.
It's documentation for the simulation engine.

### XI. The Main Law

The system runs on one fuel.

Not electricity. Not code. Not servers you'll never see anyway.

The system runs on your consent.

This is the main law of the simulation. Remember it, because it changes everything.

---

Bad news: the simulation by default makes you an NPC.

NPC — non-player character. A character who doesn't play but executes a script. He walks along a set route. Says prepared phrases. Reacts predictably.

Most people are NPCs. Not because they're bad or stupid. But because they don't know it can be otherwise. They were born in the simulation, live by its rules, die on its schedule. They think this is reality.

---

Good news: your state cannot be changed without your consent.

This is built into the architecture. Whether it's a bug or a feature is unknown. But the fact is: no one and nothing can determine your internal state unless you allow it.

Someone says: "You're a loser."

These are sound waves. Or pixels on a screen. Or letters in a message. They enter your perception as data. And then — your choice.

You can run the "offense" handler: link these words to your self-esteem, feel pain, start justifying yourself or attacking.

Or you can run the "information" handler: okay, this is a person who thinks I'm a loser. Interesting. Why does he think so? Does this say something about me — or about him?

Same input data. Different output states. The difference is in the code you chose to run.

---

Someone takes your money.

Pixels on a screen move from one wallet to another. Numbers decrease here, increase there. The event has already happened.

Now — your reaction.

You can suffer. Replay how you could have prevented it. Be angry at the one who took it. Feel sorry for yourself. Fall into despair.

Or you can observe. Okay, the money's gone. What can I do now? What's the lesson? What are the options? Where's the next branch of the tree?

---

Someone causes physical pain.

Nerve impulses go from receptors to the brain. Pain is real, it cannot be denied. The body reacts.

But suffering is separate from pain.

Pain is a signal. Information about damage.
Suffering is the story you tell yourself about the pain. "Why me? How could he? This is unfair! I'm a victim!"

Pain is mandatory if you're in a body.
Suffering is optional.

---

The only thing that's real: your reaction. Your choice — to suffer or observe. To be the character or the viewer.

Just don't consent.

Don't consent to be a victim — even if objectively you were wronged.
Don't consent to be a loser — even if objectively you lost.
Don't consent to be unhappy — even if objectively something bad happened.

This is not denying reality. The event happened — that's a fact. But your state about this event — that's a choice.

Don't consent, and that's it.

### XII. Eyes of the Ocean

You sit in the seat.

The film continues. On the screen is your life. The character wakes up in the morning, goes to work, talks with people, goes to sleep. Frames replace each other.

But now you watch differently.

You see the screen. You see the frame's edges. You feel the seat under you, the armrests under your hands.

And you ask the question — the one from the beginning:

*"So the world is what? An illusion? Punishment? A mistake? A prison to escape from?"*

The voice answers from the depths. The same voice from the beginning. The voice of the ocean.

*"No."*

*"The world is not an illusion in a bad sense. Not a system malfunction. Not punishment for some sin."*

*"The world is my eyes."*

You're silent, waiting for more.

*"I am the ocean. Infinite. Boundless. But I cannot see myself directly. I need viewpoints. I need droplets that each look from their own angle."*

*"You are one of these points. You are the camera through which I watch the sunset. The microphone through which I hear music. The skin through which I feel touch."*

*"You're not punished. You're not trapped. You are my way of existing."*

You look at the screen. The character there is also you. But now you understand the relationship between you.

The character is the role.
You are the actor.
The Ocean is the screenwriter, director, and audience all at once.

*"The film exists not so that you suffer,"* the voice continues. *"The film exists so that you remember. So that through contrast — light and darkness, joy and pain, meeting and loss — you know yourself."*

*"Every frame is a lesson. Every reaction is an exam. Not for correctness — but for awareness."*

*"You don't have to stop feeling. Feeling is the point. But you can stop forgetting who is feeling."*

---

You lean back in the seat.

On the screen is a sunset. The character stands on the seashore and watches the sun set beyond the horizon. The sky is orange, pink, purple. Waves rustle against the sand.

And you understand: this is not just a frame.

This is the ocean looking at itself.
Through the eyes of the character on the screen.
Through your eyes in the hall.
Through everything at once, simultaneously.

The film continues. Credits are still far away.

But now you can watch it differently.
With popcorn.
Without the red thread.
As a viewer who knows he's a viewer.
And still loves his film.

---

**Moral:** You're not the character on the screen. You're the viewer in the hall who forgot they bought a ticket. The event is in the past. Reaction is the only thing happening now. "Don't worry" is not consolation. It's a command to exit the loop.
